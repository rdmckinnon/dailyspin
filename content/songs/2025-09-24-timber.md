Look, I'm not going to pretend this is high art. Pitbull has never pretended that. The man samples a Lee Oskar harmonica riff, throws Ke$ha on the hook, and dares you not to dance. It's shameless. It's ridiculous. It spent three weeks at number one in fourteen countries.

"Timber" has no business working as well as it does. It's 2013 dance-pop with a harmonica riff from a 1978 instrumental track. It's hip-hop with country references. It's Ke$ha—still spelled with the dollar sign back then—yelling "It's going down!" like she's calling a square dance at the end of the world.

And yet. Put this on at the right moment, with the right people, and something happens. Call it chemistry. Call it stupidity. Call it whatever you want, but don't pretend you haven't moved to it.

Pitbull knows exactly what he is. "I have 'em like Miley Cyrus, clothes off, twerking in their bras and thongs." That's not poetry. That's inventory. He's cataloging the chaos and reporting live from the dance floor. You don't go to Pitbull for existential insight. You go because sometimes you need to let the brain rest and the body take over.

Ke$ha's chorus is the thing. That voice—half celebration, half warning. "Let's make a night you won't remember." She sells it like she's lived it, which, knowing her, she probably has.

The production does what 2013 production was supposed to do: hit hard, stay bright, never let you catch your breath long enough to ask why. Dr. Luke and Cirkut built a machine designed to make rooms full of strangers temporarily lose their minds together.

Is it disposable? Absolutely. That's the point. Not everything needs to last. Some things just need to work once, perfectly, at 1 AM when nothing else will do.

The floor fills up. The timber falls.

That's enough.
